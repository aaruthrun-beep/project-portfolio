THE CRAFT
Avani Farm Developers

I didn't start with a textbook.
I started with a broken plough.

My journey didn't start in a lab, but in the sun-hardened soil of our family farm.

It began with the **Vale Kuttu**, a manual multi-tool my father designed and we built together. It was where I first learned that engineering is fundamentally about necessity —building the tools you need when the world doesn't provide them. This vision, which we call **Avani Farm Developers**, is the overarching mission of my work: using the precision of modern electronics to solve the brutal challenges of physical labor.

“He was someone who sincerely wished for my success in his own quiet way. He didn't always express it, but his deep appreciation and belief in my path carry me forward today.”

— A Tribute to a Mentor

Growing up in a farming family, machines were never "concepts" to me —they were the tools we depended on. I've been at the root of the problem. I've felt the vibration of the hardware and the strain of the tines. This is why I chose a **Diploma** path; I wanted to touch the metal and understand the mechanics long before I touched a line of code.

Today, I work across the three pillars: **Electrical, Mechanical, and Software**. I do this individually not to "do everything," but because a real machine doesn't respect departments. If a robotics system is going to survive the mud and heat of the field, it needs an architect who understands the soil as well as the silicon.

Indoctrination

Nikola Tesla once warned that when education limits imagination, it becomes indoctrination. I chose a different path — one that values the "fire to be kindled" over rigid, controlling systems that often stifle true progress. I am no longer defined by separate hardware or software disciplines; for me, they have fused into a singular organism of logic. My only indoctrination is to the **discipline of the craft** and the reclamation of imagination through the grit of the machine.
